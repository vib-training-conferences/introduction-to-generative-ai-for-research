# Exploratory Analysis of Articles Using NotebookLM

Perform exploratory analyses on scientific articles and related documents by using **NotebookLM**. 
This exercise will help you organize information, identify key concepts, and visualize relationships between ideas.|

Time: 10 min
Grouping: individual activity

## Step 1: Collect Source Material
Perform a search using a tool of your choice (e.g., AI tool of choice, or Google Scholar, PubMed) to find **three articles or documents** relevant to your research topic.  
Requirements:
- Files must have an **open license** (e.g., CC-BY).
- If you cannot find suitable files, use the provided example documents.

## Step 2: Upload to NotebookLM
- Create a **new notebook** in NotebookLM.
- Upload the three selected files.
- Ensure the documents are properly indexed so NotebookLM can process them.

## Step 3: Generate a Mind Map
- Use NotebookLM’s summarization and visualization features to create a **mind map** that:
  - Highlights the main concepts from the documents.
- Take a moment to reflect on how this visualization supports exploratory analysis.

1. The “Gap Hunter” Prompt

When you’re new to a topic, knowing what is already known and what is at the forefront of academic research can be a challenge. A clearly identified research gap can help you make your paper more impactful and relevant. Here is the prompt:

Prompt:
```
You are an expert research mentor specializing in [discipline X]. From the following sources, identify at least three categories of research gaps: (a) methodological, (b) theoretical, and (c) empirical/contextual. For each category, write: (1) a one-sentence description of the gap, (2) an example paper or finding that highlights the gap, and (3) a suggested research question. Present as a table with citations.
```

2. The “Methodology Deep Dive” Prompt

When discussing limitations over fields and previous shortcomings, it is important to know what has been done insufficiently and point that clearly out in your introduction and discussion sections. This prompt can help:

Prompt:
```
Analyze the methodological weaknesses and recurring challenges in the field of [discipline/topic X]. From the provided sources, extract 4–6 common issues (e.g., small sample sizes, weak controls, measurement biases, reproducibility concerns). For each issue, explain (1) how it appears in multiple papers, (2) why it matters for validity or generalizability, and (3) what solutions or best practices could address it. Conclude with a short synthesis: which weaknesses most seriously limit progress in [topic X], and how future research could overcome them.
```

3. The “Recurring Topics Extractor” Prompt

When diving into a new field of research, it is essential to understand the single concepts that researchers are studying and how they relate to one another. Each idea can later become a note in your note-taking vault, but identifying them can be time-consuming, especially for young career researchers. This prompt can help you determine the key topics right away and streamline your note-taking and reading process:

Prompt:
```
From the provided papers on [topic X], identify the 5–10 most recurring themes, concepts, or research questions. For each theme, provide: (1) a short definition in your own words, (2) which papers mention it (with citations), and (3) a one-sentence summary of how it is treated (e.g., debated, assumed, tested). Present the output as a table or structured bullet list I can use as a basis for targeted note-taking.
```

4. The “Trend Detection” Prompt

When writing a literature review, it is incredibly useful to understand how the field has developed in recent years, which allows you to place your research more clearly and will help polish the introduction, making it more impactful and your paper more relevant. Try out this prompt:

Prompt:
```
Analyze the provided collection of papers on [topic X]. Identify 5–7 major trends over time (e.g., rising methods, shifting focus areas, new theoretical debates). For each trend, provide: (1) a short description, (2) the approximate timeframe when it became more frequent, (3) example papers or citations, and (4) whether the trend appears to be increasing, stable, or declining. Conclude with a short synthesis of what these trends suggest for the future of research in [topic X]. Present results as a timeline-style bullet list.
```

5. The “Contradiction Finder” Prompt

A hallmark of a good literature review is identifying where insights lead to contradictions.

Prompt:
```
From the provided papers on [topic X], identify major contradictions or conflicting findings. For each contradiction, provide: (1) the specific claim or result from each side (quoted or paraphrased with citations), (2) possible reasons for the disagreement (e.g., differences in method, sample, context, or assumptions), and (3) a short note on what kind of further evidence would help resolve the conflict. Present as a structured bullet list or a two-column table contrasting the opposing findings.
```
